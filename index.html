Abstract

Cyber-attacks are increasing, and organisations must remain constantly alert. Security Operations Centres (SOCs) sit at the frontline of this defence, responsible for monitoring, detecting, and responding to incidents. This article explores the SOC analyst’s role in the detection and prevention of cyber-attacks. Drawing on recent research, it outlines how detection processes, technical mechanisms, human factors, and artificial intelligence shape day-to-day operations. It concludes that meaningful cyber-resilience still depends on skilled analysts who can combine automation with judgement rather than on technology alone.

Introduction

As organisations expand their digital infrastructure, cyber security has become a major operational challenge. The SOC acts as the command centre for cyber defence, where analysts continuously monitor networks, identify suspicious activity, and coordinate responses. They interpret automated alerts, separate genuine threats from background noise, and protect digital assets under significant time pressure. Their work blends automation with human insight to deliver both reactive incident response and proactive defence. An effective SOC therefore relies as much on organisational discipline and situational awareness as on tools and platforms.

Analysis and Discussion

Effective SOC performance depends on the interaction of people, processes, and technology. Analysts typically work in tiers based on experience: junior analysts handle initial triage and remove obvious false positives, while senior analysts investigate complex incidents and lead threat-hunting activities. Conn (2022) describes the SOC as a cyber “war room”, where rapid collaboration and clear decision-making are essential for an effective response.

Detection begins with collecting alerts from firewalls, intrusion-detection systems, and endpoint tools, all of which feed into a SIEM platform that correlates events. However, Alahmadi, Axon and Martinovic (2022) highlight that up to 99% of alerts can be false positives, creating analyst fatigue and increasing the risk that genuine threats are missed. To manage this, analysts validate alerts using log analysis, context checks, and external threat intelligence.

Machine-learning approaches support this process. Shaik et al. (2022) show that datasets such as NSL-KDD can improve detection accuracy, while Yang et al. (2021) propose ASSERT, a near real-time learning model that helps identify attack behaviour more reliably. Despite these advances, human judgement remains central because analysts must interpret incomplete information and assess the potential impact of each alert.

Traditional SOC performance metrics—such as Mean Time to Detect and Mean Time to Respond— only partially measure effectiveness. Forsberg et al. (2023) argue that investigation depth and decision-quality are equally important. Agyepong et al. (2023) further suggest that analyst performance should account for cognitive workload, as high alert volumes significantly affect accuracy and well-being.

Human factors also influence SOC outcomes. Alahmadi et al. (2022) note that analysts often develop “trust hierarchies” that favour certain tools, potentially creating blind spots. Majid et al. (2021) observe that successful SOCs share strong governance structures, supportive leadership, and continuous knowledge exchange. Beyond responding to alerts, analysts contribute to prevention by conducting threat-hunting investigations and feeding lessons from incidents into improved monitoring rules and security controls. Patterson et al. (2023) warn that organisations often fail to document lessons learned, limiting long-term improvement.

Automation is increasingly embedded into SOC workflows. SOAR platforms can block malicious IPs, isolate compromised endpoints, and perform routine enrichment automatically, reducing repetitive manual tasks. More recently, Singh et al. (2025) show that large language models can assist analysts by summarising incidents and highlighting relevant indicators, although expert oversight remains essential. Aslan et al. (2022) reinforce that despite advanced automation, accountability and human interpretation remain fundamental principles of cybersecurity.

Overall, SOC analysts remain central to organisational defence. Their analytical skills and situational awareness connect technical signals with strategic action. While alert fatigue, complex hybrid environments, and evolving threats pose challenges, well-supported analysts operating within mature SOCs significantly reduce cyber-risk. Effective cybersecurity ultimately relies on a partnership between skilled human analysts and well-designed technical controls, rather than automation alone.
