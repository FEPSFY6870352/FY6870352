The SOC Analyst’s Role in the Detection and Prevention of Cyber Attacks

Abstract

The increasing sophistication of cyber-attacks has positioned Security Operations Centres (SOCs) as an essential component of organisational defence. This paper explores the role of SOC analysts in detecting, analysing, and preventing cyber threats, emphasising the balance between human expertise and automated security technologies. Alert fatigue, false positives, system complexity, and evolving threat actors present ongoing challenges. Recent literature highlights the importance of threat intelligence, machine learning, and large language models as supportive tools for enhancing detection accuracy and reducing analyst workload. However, automation alone cannot replace human interpretation and decision-making. Effective cyber defence therefore depends on integrating advanced automated systems with trained analysts who can interpret ambiguous data, assess risk, and coordinate strategic responses.

Introduction

As digital infrastructure expands, cybersecurity has become a major organisational concern. Security Operations Centres (SOCs) function as the command hub for cyber defence, where analysts continuously monitor systems, validate alerts, investigate suspicious behaviour, and initiate incident response. While automated tools detect anomalies, analysts play a crucial role in interpreting incomplete data, identifying genuine threats, and determining appropriate actions. Modern SOC environments require cooperation between human reasoning and automated security technologies, creating a socio-technical system that must adapt to evolving threats.

Analysts face increasing pressure as cyber-attacks become more complex and interconnected. Cloud services, IoT devices, and hybrid networks have widened the attack surface. As a result, SOC personnel must not only understand technical alerts but also analyse broader behavioural patterns that indicate compromise.

Analysis and Discussion

Security Operations Centres depend on a structured workflow consisting of people, tools, and defined processes. Analysts are divided into several tiers: Tier 1 provides initial triage of alerts, Tier 2 performs in-depth investigations, and Tier 3 handles advanced threat hunting and complex incident response coordination. This tiered structure supports efficient division of labour during high alert volumes.

A major SOC challenge is alert fatigue. SIEM tools often generate thousands of alerts daily, many of which are false positives. Excessive noise leads to burnout, slower response times, and missed threats. Improving SIEM rule tuning, integrating threat intelligence, and using behaviour-based detection models are essential to reducing false alerts.

Human factors significantly influence SOC performance. Analysts rely on communication, critical thinking, and collaboration to validate evidence and reach conclusions. Bias can affect decision-making when analysts rely heavily on certain tools. Strong governance, documentation, and training reduce these issues and improve accuracy.

Threat hunting represents a proactive SOC activity where analysts search for indicators of compromise before alerts are triggered. This requires knowledge of attacker techniques, behavioural patterns, and the organisation’s normal network behaviour.

Automation using SOAR systems has become increasingly important. Automating repetitive tasks—such as isolating hosts, blocking malicious IPs, or generating tickets—allows analysts to focus on higher-level analysis. However, automation must be supervised, as tools cannot understand business context or interpret subtle attacker behaviour.

Machine learning models help detect anomalies, cluster alerts, and prioritise high-risk events. Large Language Models (LLMs) assist analysts by summarising logs and producing reports. These tools improve efficiency but require human oversight to prevent errors or hallucinations. The literature agrees that analysts remain the core of SOC operations: tools provide speed and scale, while humans provide judgement, intuition, and context.
