Abstract

Cyber-attacks are increasing, and organisations must remain constantly alert. Security Operations Centres (SOCs) sit at the frontline of this defence, responsible for monitoring, detecting, and responding to incidents. This article explores the SOC analyst’s role in the detection and prevention of cyber-attacks. Drawing on recent research, it outlines how detection processes, technical mechanisms, human factors, and artificial intelligence shape day-to-day operations. It concludes that meaningful cyber-resilience still depends on skilled analysts who can combine automation with judgement rather than on technology alone.

Introduction

As organisations expand their digital infrastructure, cyber security has become a major operational challenge. The SOC acts as the command centre for cyber defence, where analysts continuously monitor networks, identify suspicious activity, and coordinate responses. They interpret automated alerts, separate genuine threats from background noise, and protect digital assets under significant time pressure. Their work blends automation with human insight to deliver both reactive incident response and proactive defence. An effective SOC therefore relies as much on organisational discipline and situational awareness as on tools and platforms.

Analysis and Discussion

Effective SOC performance rests on the interaction of people, processes, and technology. Analysts are commonly organised into tiers according to experience. Junior analysts handle first-line triage and filter obvious false positives, while more experienced analysts investigate complex incidents and senior staff lead threat hunting and response. Conn (2022) compares the SOC to a cyber war room in which rapid decision-making and clear communication are essential; the combined judgement of each analyst shapes the speed and quality of the overall response.

Detection typically begins with the continuous collection of events from intrusion-detection systems, firewalls, and endpoint security products. These data streams are ingested by a Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) platform, which correlates logs and raises alerts when suspicious patterns are observed. However, Alahmadi, Axon and Martinovic (2022) report that up to 99% of alarms in some SOCs are false positives, contributing to analyst fatigue and the danger that genuine attacks are overlooked.

To manage this, analysts validate alarms by checking internal logs, consulting external threat-intelligence sources, and assessing whether activity fits normal behaviour. Shaik et al. (2022) show that using labelled datasets such as NSL-KDD with data-driven algorithms can improve intrusion-detection precision, while Yang et al. (2021) propose ASSERT, a model that learns attack behaviour in near real time to help correlate complex alert streams. Even so, human interpretation remains central: analysts must work with incomplete information, apply contextual reasoning, and choose proportionate responses.

Traditional performance metrics such as Mean Time to Detect and Mean Time to Respond do not fully capture analytical quality. Forsberg et al. (2023) argue that SOC effectiveness should also consider investigation depth and decision-making. Agyepong et al. (2023) therefore suggest measuring performance as a balance between speed, accuracy, and cognitive workload, acknowledging that constant high alert volumes impose a significant burden on staff.

Human and organisational factors further influence SOC outcomes. Alahmadi et al. (2022) observe that analysts often develop “trust hierarchies” that favour familiar tools, which may bias investigations if alternative sources are ignored. Majid et al. (2021) find that mature SOCs are characterised by supportive leadership, clear governance, and strong knowledge sharing. Beyond reacting to alerts, analysts also contribute to prevention by conducting threat-hunting activities that search for previously unknown compromises. Insights from incidents feed into improved controls, monitoring rules, and security architecture, although Patterson et al. (2023) note that organisations frequently fail to capture lessons learned in a systematic way.

Automation and artificial intelligence increasingly shape SOC workflows. Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response (SOAR) platforms can automatically isolate suspected endpoints or block malicious IP addresses, reducing repetitive manual effort. More recently, Singh et al. (2025) show that large language models can support analysts by summarising incident data and drafting reports, but their outputs still require expert validation. Aslan et al. (2022) emphasise that, even with sophisticated automation, core principles of availability, integrity, and confidentiality demand accountable human oversight.

Overall, the literature presents SOC analysts as the cognitive core of modern cyber defence. Their analytical skills, adaptability, and situational awareness connect technical signals with strategic response. Although they face challenges such as alert fatigue, complex infrastructures, and evolving attack techniques, trained analysts working within well-governed SOCs can significantly reduce organisational risk. Good cybersecurity therefore depends on an effective partnership between human expertise and technical controls, rather than on technology in isolation, and the SOC analyst’s role remains central to maintaining resilience in the digital era.
