Abstract

The rise of sophisticated cyber-attacks has increased reliance on Security Operations Centres (SOCs) as the frontline of organisational defence. This paper examines the role of SOC analysts in detecting and preventing cyber threats by combining automated systems with human expertise. Drawing on recent studies, it highlights challenges such as alert fatigue, false positives, increasing system complexity, and the expanding responsibilities of human analysts. The research also evaluates how emerging technologies, including machine learning and large language models, influence modern SOC operations. The study concludes that effective cyber defence is achieved through the balance of automation, situational awareness, experience, and collaborative problem-solving among analysts.

Introduction

As organisations continue to expand their digital infrastructure, cybersecurity has emerged as one of the defining challenges of the modern era. Security Operations Centres serve as mission control for cyber defence, where analysts continuously monitor, detect, and respond to threats in real time. According to Aslan et al. (2022):contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}, SOCs provide critical defensive capabilities by correlating logs, identifying anomalies, and investigating alerts generated from a variety of security tools. Analysts play a crucial role in interpreting incomplete information, prioritising incidents, and ensuring operational resilience. Within a SOC, automation and human judgement work together. While tools such as intrusion detection systems and SIEMs generate alerts, analysts must validate their accuracy, determine the severity of incidents, and decide whether a real threat exists. SOC effectiveness depends not only on technology but on disciplined procedures, communication, and the analytical abilities of its personnel.

Analysis and discussion

Security Operations Centres rely on the combined strength of people, processes, and technology. Analysts are typically organised into tiered structures where Tier 1 personnel triage alerts, Tier 2 analysts investigate incidents, and senior analysts perform threat hunting and coordinate responses. Conn (2022):contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1} compares SOC operations to “war rooms” where fast decision-making and collaboration determine success during critical incidents. Modern SOCs are built around continuous data collection. Logs from firewalls, intrusion detection systems, endpoints, and cloud environments feed into SIEM platforms for correlation. However, Alahmadi, Axon and Martinovic (2022):contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2} discovered that up to 99% of SIEM alarms are false positives—an issue that contributes heavily to analyst fatigue. This creates a need for improved filtering, triage strategies, and threat intelligence integration. Machine learning methods offer potential improvements. Shaik et al. (2022):contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3} showed that datasets such as NSL-KDD can significantly enhance intrusion detection accuracy. Yang et al. (2021):contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4} developed ASSERT, a near real-time learning model capable of identifying attack behaviours dynamically. Despite technological advances, Forsberg et al. (2023):contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5} note that MTTD and MTTR alone cannot measure decision-making quality, emphasising the human role within SOC processes. Bias is also a challenge. Analysts often rely more heavily on tools they trust, creating “trust hierarchies” that may prevent the adoption of better methods (Alahmadi et al., 2022):contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}. Collaboration and training can reduce this effect. Majid et al. (2021):contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7} highlight that SOCs supported by strong governance and knowledge-sharing perform more effectively in crisis situations. Threat hunting is another essential component. Analysts proactively search for unknown threats, guided by behavioural patterns and previous incidents. Patterson et al. (2023):contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8} observe that insufficient documentation of lessons learned reduces long-term defensive effectiveness. AI-driven automation is expanding within SOCs. SOAR systems streamline repetitive tasks like isolating compromised hosts or blocking malicious IPs. Singh et al. (2025):contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9} found that large language models support analysts by summarising incidents and generating reports, though human validation remains essential to prevent errors. Automation enhances efficiency but cannot replace the contextual awareness, intuition, and judgement of skilled analysts.

Overall, the literature demonstrates that SOC analysts remain the cognitive core of cyber defence. Despite advances in automation, organisational security depends on human expertise, experience, intuition, and communication. Analysts act as the bridge between technology and strategic response—interpreting ambiguous data, coordinating with stakeholders, and strengthening security posture through continuous improvement.
