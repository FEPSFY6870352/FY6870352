Abstract

The rise of sophisticated cyber-attacks has increased reliance on Security Operations Centres (SOCs) as the frontline of organisational defence. This paper examines the role of SOC analysts in detecting and preventing cyber threats through a combination of automated systems and human expertise. Challenges such as alert fatigue, false positives, and expanding system complexity are explored, alongside the influence of machine learning and large language models on SOC performance. The findings highlight that effective cyber defence relies on balancing automation with the experience, judgement, and situational awareness of analysts.

Introduction

As organisations expand their digital infrastructure, cybersecurity has become one of the defining challenges of the modern era. Security Operations Centres act as the command hub for cyber defence, where teams of analysts continuously monitor, detect, and respond to threats. SOC analysts interpret alerts, correlate logs, evaluate anomalies, and make rapid decisions that directly impact organisational security.

While automated systems generate alerts, human analysts assess severity, confirm malicious intent, and determine the correct response. This combination of technological capability and human analysis forms the backbone of modern cyber defence. Effective SOC operations rely on communication, process discipline, and continual learning.

Analysis and discussion

SOCs depend on a layered structure of people, processes, and technology. Analysts are typically organised into tiers: Tier 1 handles triage, Tier 2 investigates complex incidents, and Tier 3 conducts threat hunting and long-term strategic analysis. This system ensures efficiency during high-volume events.

SOCs collect data from multiple sources: firewalls, endpoint systems, cloud logs, intrusion detection systems, and identity providers. However, the overwhelming number of false positives generated by SIEM tools contributes to alert fatigue. This has pushed organisations to adopt smarter filtering approaches and integrate threat intelligence feeds to improve triage accuracy.

Machine learning systems offer opportunities to reduce analyst workload. They can detect behavioural anomalies, cluster similar alerts, and identify high-risk events. Despite technological progress, research shows that algorithmic models cannot fully replace human expertise. Analysts provide contextual knowledge, creative problem-solving, and risk-based decision-making.

Analyst bias presents another operational challenge. Personal familiarity with certain tools may cause over-reliance on them, affecting judgement. Collaborative environments supported by clear governance and documentation help mitigate this issue. Additionally, strong knowledge-sharing practices build team resilience.

Threat hunting is a proactive SOC activity where analysts actively search for hidden or emerging threats. This includes analysing unusual user behaviour, scanning for lateral movement, and reviewing historical logs for patterns that automated systems may overlook.

Automation—especially through SOAR tools—now supports SOCs by handling repetitive tasks such as IP blocking, host isolation, and automated ticket creation. Large language models have also emerged as support tools, helping analysts summarise incidents and produce reports. However, these systems still require human oversight to maintain accuracy and prevent misinterpretation.

Overall, research concludes that SOC analysts remain the intellectual centre of cyber defence. Despite increasing automation, the effectiveness of a SOC ultimately depends on human judgement, experience, and coordination.
